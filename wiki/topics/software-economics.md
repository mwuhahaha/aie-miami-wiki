## Overview

Software economics is the topic connecting Geoffrey Huntley’s AI Engineer Miami 2026 talk, [[talks/2026-04-20-geoffrey-huntley-software-development-now-costs-less-than-minimum-wage|Software Development Now Costs Less Than Minimum Wage]], to the practical tooling now reshaping engineering work. In this context, the subject is not abstract cost theory: it is about what happens when AI-assisted development environments such as [[tools/cursor|Cursor]] make the everyday loop of reading code, changing code, running it, and reviewing results dramatically cheaper and faster.

## Why It Matters Here

The Miami materials frame this topic through [[people/geoffrey-huntley|Geoffrey Huntley]], affiliated with [[companies/latent-patterns|Latent Patterns]], and his argument that software development has crossed a major price threshold. The linked talk title makes the claim concrete: development work can now cost less than minimum wage when model-assisted coding systems are applied to routine engineering tasks. That shifts the economic question from whether software can be built to how teams should allocate judgment, review, coordination, and product taste when implementation itself becomes much less expensive.

## Notes

This topic is closely tied to agentic development practice rather than just AI hype. [[tools/cursor|Cursor]] matters here because it represents the kind of toolchain that changes the cost structure: engineers can stay inside the edit-run-review workflow while delegating more of the mechanical coding labor to models. Geoffrey Huntley’s session appears to use that shift as evidence for a broader software economics argument: cheaper code generation changes staffing assumptions, delivery timelines, and the value of human oversight.
